Chhath is an ancient Hindu festival historically celebrated in the state of Uttar Pradesh in northern India. It is also observed in Nepal and Bangladesh. The festival commemorates the death of the Hindu god Shiva and his consort Parvati.  The festival is celebrated on the third day after the new moon of Kartika month (September-October). It is a time for family reunion, thanksgiving, and prayer. The day is also marked by a special congregational ceremony called the 'char Dham'. During the ceremony, pilgrims offer prayers to Shiva and Parvati at four important pilgrimage sites - Mahabaleshwar, Renuka Yatra at Kashi Vishwanath, Sasthamkotta and Ganges Sagar.  Chhath is one of the most important festivals of Hindus. It is celebrated with great fervor all over India and Nepal. The significance of this festival goes back to antiquity. Chhath originated from an event that took place when Lord Shiva killed the buffalo demon Mahishasura. As a result of this event, Goddess Parvati was very upset with her husband. In order to placate her, Lord Shiva placed his locks of hair on four rivers - Ganga, Yamuna, Saraswati and Godavari - as a sign of his humility. On the third day after the new moon of Kartika month (September-October), Hindus observe this festival to pay their respects to Lord Shiva and Goddess Parvati.
