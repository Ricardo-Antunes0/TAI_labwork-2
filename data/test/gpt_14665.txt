Lublin is the ninth-largest city in Poland, with a population of 404,612. It is the seat of Lublin Voivodeship, in eastern Poland. Lublin is also the largest city in the Lublin Warmian-Masurian Voivodeship and the fourth-largest city in the country overall. The city was founded in the early 14th century by King Władysław I the Elbow-High, on the site of an earlier wooden settlement and it became an important center of trade and culture in the region. Lublin was occupied by Nazi Germany during World War II and was liberated by the Red Army on 27 January 1945. It became part of Poland again after World War II and has since been developing as a major tourist center and industrial center. Lublin is one of Poland's oldest cities, founded by King Władysław I the Elbow-High in 1334 as a town on the territory of Lwów (now Lviv, Ukraine). The city's name derives from its founder: Lublin (Polish: Łublinek) is a Slavic name meaning "little wolf", which may have been given to it because of its location near many wolves' dens.
