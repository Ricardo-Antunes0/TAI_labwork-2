François-Émile de Lansac (1 October 1803  – 10 May 1868) was a French chemist, physicist, and mathematician who made significant contributions to the fields of thermodynamics and statistical mechanics. He is also known for his work on heat engines and the theory of elasticity.  Lansac was born in Lyon, France on 1 October 1803. After studying at the colleges of Lyon and Paris, he entered the École Polytechnique in 1830. He became professor of mathematics at the École Polytechnique in 1852, and professor of physics in 1857. He served as president of the French Academy of Sciences from 1865 to 1868.  Lansac made important contributions to the fields of thermodynamics and statistical mechanics. He is also known for his work on heat engines and the theory of elasticity. His most famous work is probably his study of the Second Law of Thermodynamics, which he published in 1849.
