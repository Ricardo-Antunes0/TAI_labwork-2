Frank Leslie Cross  (22 January 1900 – 4 December 1988) was an American anthropologist and social scientist who specialized in the study of cultural variation. He was professor of anthropology at the University of Chicago from 1933 until his retirement in 1968.  Cross was born on 22 January 1900 in Omaha, Nebraska, to Emma (née Hopkins) and Frank Leslie Cross. He attended the University of Nebraska-Lincoln, where he received his B.A. in 1921 and his M.A. in 1922. He then attended Yale University, where he received his Ph.D. in 1926.  Cross began his teaching career at the University of Chicago as an instructor in anthropology in 1933. He became a full professor there in 1948 and served as dean of the faculty from 1955 to 1958. Cross retired from the University of Chicago in 1968 but continued to serve on various university committees until his death on 4 December 1988.  Cross's work primarily focused on cultural variation, with a particular focus on cross-cultural comparisons and similarities between different societies. His notable works include "The Cross-Cultural Method" (1946), "The Social Organization of Culture" (1951), "Culture and Social Change" (1961), "The Anatomy of Cultural Forms" (1965), and "Anthropology Today" (1972). Cross was also a prolific writer, publishing over 60 books and articles during his lifetime. He was awarded the National Medal of Science in 1962 and the Presidential Medal of Freedom in 1968.
