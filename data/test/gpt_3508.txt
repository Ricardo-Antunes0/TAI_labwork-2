Robert Lincoln Drew (February 15, 1924 – July 8, 2001) was an American journalist and a pioneer of the investigative reporting movement. He is best known for his work as the founding executive producer and head of the investigations unit at ABC News in the 1960s.  Drew was born in New York City, the son of Lincoln Drew, a lawyer and Democratic politician who served as United States Ambassador to Poland, and Edith (née Lincoln), a socialite. His paternal grandfather was Civil War general Robert E. Lee. He attended Harvard University, where he was a member of the Scroll and Key Society and the Hasty Pudding Club.  After graduating from Harvard, Drew took a job as a reporter at The Miami Herald. In 1951, he joined CBS News as a reporter and became one of its leading reporters on investigative stories. In 1960, he left CBS to become the founding executive producer and head of the investigations unit at ABC News. He led ABC's coverage of major news stories including the assassination of President John F. Kennedy and the Vietnam War.  Drew retired from ABC News in 1981 after suffering a stroke. He died in 2001 at the age of 78.
