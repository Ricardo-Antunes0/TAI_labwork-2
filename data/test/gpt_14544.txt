Louis-Michel Viger (September 28, 1785 – May 15, 1863) was a lawyer, political figure in Lower Canada and the first Lieutenant Governor of Quebec.  Born in Quebec City, Viger studied law at the Université Laval. He was called to the Quebec bar in 1807 and set up a practice in Quebec City. In 1816, he was appointed King's Counsel. In 1820, he married Marie-Anne-Françoise-Angélique de Lorimier.  Viger entered politics in 1820 when he was elected to the Legislative Assembly of Lower Canada as a representative for Quebec City. He served as speaker for the assembly from 1824 to 1830. In 1830, he was appointed attorney general for Lower Canada. He served in that position until 1834, when he was appointed lieutenant governor of Lower Canada. In this position, Viger played a major role in the development of Lower Canada and helped to strengthen ties between the colony and the British government. He served as lieutenant governor until 1840.  Viger retired from politics in 1840 and returned to his legal practice. He died in Quebec City at the age of 78.
