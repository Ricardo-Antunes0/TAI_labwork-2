George Alfred Lohmann (2 June 1865 – 24 October 1936) was a German physician who is considered the founder of modern cardiology. He was the first to describe the Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome and the first to use an electrocardiogram (ECG) to diagnose heart disease.  Lohmann was born in Halle an der Saale, Saxony-Anhalt, on 2 June 1865. He studied medicine at the University of Berlin and at the University of Zurich, where he received his doctorate in 1892. That same year, he began working as a physician at the Berlin Charité Hospital. In 1899, he founded the Berlin Cardiovascular Clinic, which became one of Europe's leading cardiovascular centers.  Lohmann is best known for his work on cardiology. He is credited with describing the Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome, which is a condition that affects the electrical activity of the heart muscle. He also pioneered the use of an ECG to diagnose heart disease. Lohmann died on 24 October 1936 in Berlin.
