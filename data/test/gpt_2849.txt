Badr ibn ʿAbdallāh al-Ḥammāmī  also known as al-Ḥammāmī Badr or Abu Musab al-Zarqāwī, was a Muslim commander and military leader during the Abbasid Caliphate.  Badr al-Hammami was born in 795 AH (1298 AD) in the city of Zarqawa, located in the eastern province of Iraq. He belonged to the Banu Musa tribe and was originally from the town of Hammam al-Alil. He first came to prominence during the reign of Caliph al-Musta'in (reigned 833-842 AD), serving as a general under the command of Jafar al-Sadiq. During the latter's campaign against the Byzantines in Egypt, Badr distinguished himself at the Battle of Marj Dabiq, where he routed an imperial army led by Constantine Lekapenos. For his actions, Badr was promoted to governor of Damascus and Aleppo.  In 845 AD, Caliph al-Mutawakkil appointed Badr as chief of police in Baghdad. This post gave him a strong foothold in Abbasid political affairs, and he used it to consolidate his powerbase. In 850 AD, he became governor of Fars Province, and two years later he was made head of security for Baghdad.
