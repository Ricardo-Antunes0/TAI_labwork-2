Edward Herbert Wright (September 28, 1863 in Lexington, Massachusetts – December 4, 1936 in Lexington) was an American philosopher and educational reformer. Wright is best known for his work on education, most notably his book The Philosophy of Education (1902). He also wrote on aesthetics, ethics, religion, and social philosophy.  Wright was born in Lexington, Massachusetts to Edward Wright and Lydia (née Morse) Wright. He attended the University of Michigan from 1883 to 1886, where he studied mathematics and philosophy. He then transferred to Harvard University, where he completed his undergraduate degree in 1888 and his graduate degree in 1892.  After completing his graduate studies, Wright spent a year working as a research mathematician for the National Bureau of Economic Research. He then spent five years teaching mathematics at the University of Chicago before becoming a professor at the University of Iowa in 1907. Wright remained at Iowa until his retirement in 1934.  Wright was a member of the American Philosophical Society, the American Academy of Arts and Sciences, and the National Academy of Sciences. He was also a recipient of the Guggenheim Fellowship and the Carnegie Fellowship.  Edward Herbert Wright died in Lexington on December 4, 1936.
