He is most famous for his work in the field of twelve-tone music, and for his book, The Technique of Twelve-Tone Composers.  Irving Shuman was born in Niles, Michigan, to Jewish parents. He studied at the University of Michigan, where he earned his Bachelor's degree in 1918. He then studied at the University of Vienna with Arnold Schoenberg and Anton Webern. He returned to the United States in 1922 and became a professor at Yale University. He remained at Yale until his retirement in 1966.  Shuman's compositions include orchestral works, chamber music, choral works, and solo piano works. His best-known works are the orchestral suites Pacific 231 and the Concerto for Violin and Orchestra. He also wrote several operas, including The Great American Dream (1948), which was based on the life of Andrew Carnegie.  Shuman was awarded the Pulitzer Prize for Music in 1959. In 1978 he was awarded the National Medal of Arts.
