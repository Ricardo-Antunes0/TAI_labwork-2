Born in the town of Voronezh, in southern Russia, Brukhonenko studied at the Voronezh School of Painting and Sculpture from 1907 to 1911. He then went to the Kiev Art School (1912-1915), where he was taught by Lev Khokhlov and Pavel Klimt. In 1915, he moved to Petrograd, where he became a member of the group of artists known as the Peredvizhniki. In 1916, he participated in the First World War and was wounded in the leg. After the war, he returned to Kiev and continued his studies at the Kiev Academy of Fine Arts (1920-1924). In 1924, Brukhonenko moved to Moscow and became a full member of the Association of Artists of Revolutionary Russia. In 1925, he participated in the fourth Moscow Biennale. From 1925 to 1928, he taught at the Surikov Moscow State Academic Institute of Painting, Sculpture and Architecture. In 1929, he was named an assistant professor at that institute.  Brukhonenko is best known for his paintings of peasant life and landscapes. His works are noted for their naturalistic style and use of bright colors.
