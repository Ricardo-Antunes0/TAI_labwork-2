Philip Henry Moore (February 22, 1799 – September 14, 1872) was an American artist who specialized in landscape painting. He was a leading figure in the Hudson River School and one of the most renowned painters of the mid-19th century.  Moore was born in Albany, New York, on February 22, 1799, the son of Philip Moore and Lucy (née Roe) Moore. His father was a successful lawyer and judge who served as New York State Attorney General from 1814 to 1817. Moore attended the University of Pennsylvania, where he studied law and art under Benjamin West. He moved to New York City in 1819 and began to work as a draftsman for an engraving house. He exhibited his first paintings in 1825 at the National Academy of Design, and two years later he moved to West Point, New York, where he became a professor of painting and drawing.  In 1829 Moore married Eliza Houghton; they had four children. Moore traveled extensively during the 1830s, painting scenes of Europe, North Africa, and the Middle East. In 1845 he returned to the United States, settling first in Buffalo, New York, and then in Hudson River town of Poughkeepsie, New York. There he built a large house and became one of its principal residents.  Moore's paintings are distinguished by their restrained coloration and use of light and shadow to create a sense of depth.
