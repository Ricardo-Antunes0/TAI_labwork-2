James Forman (October 4, 1928 – January 12, 2010) was an American playwright, screenwriter, and actor. He is best known for his plays A Streetcar Named Desire and Cat on a Hot Tin Roof.  He was born in New Orleans, Louisiana, the son of Allie (née Brown), a nurse, and James Forman Sr., an insurance salesman. His paternal grandparents were Czech and his maternal grandparents were Irish. He was raised in the Roman Catholic faith. He had one sister, Barbara.  He attended Louisiana State University and later the Yale School of Drama, where he studied under Elia Kazan. After graduating from Yale in 1951, he moved to New York City to try to become a playwright.  Forman's first play, "A Streetcar Named Desire", opened on Broadway in 1951 and won him the Pulitzer Prize for Drama. The play was later adapted for the screen and directed by Kazan; it became one of the most acclaimed films of all time.  Following "Streetcar", Forman wrote "Cat on a Hot Tin Roof" (1958), which also received critical acclaim and won him his second Pulitzer Prize for Drama. He then wrote "A Cheap Imitation of Life" (1963), "The Unmade Bed" (1964), "The Maids" (1965), "The Crucible" (1996), and "Long Day's Journey into Night" (1996).
