Grosvenor Arundell Francis (14 August 1873 – 17 July 1947) was an English banker, business magnate and Conservative politician who served as the Chancellor of the Exchequer in the Lloyd George coalition government of 1918-1922.  Born in 1873 into a wealthy banking family, Francis made his fortune through his investments in coal and steel businesses. He joined the Conservative Party in 1911, and served as President of the Board of Trade from 1922 to 1924. In 1918, he was appointed Chancellor of the Exchequer by Prime Minister David Lloyd George, a position he held until 1922. Francis was a key figure in the implementation of the Liberal budget of 1919, which introduced measures such as the National Insurance Act and the Unemployment Insurance Act. He also played a role in the negotiation of the Treaty of Sèvres, which ended World War I.  After leaving office, Francis became a senior figure in British business. He served on the boards of several major companies, and was chairman of Associated British Ports from 1939 to 1946. He died in 1947 at the age of 79.
