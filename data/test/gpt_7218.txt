Peggy Tsiang Cherng (pronounced Chur-ng, born 1947/1948) is an American academic and activist who has been actively involved in the dissident movement in China since the 1970s.  Cherng earned her undergraduate degree from Wellesley College in 1967 and her masters and PhD degrees from Harvard University in 1971 and 1975, respectively. After completing her education, she began her career as a research associate at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology's Center for International Studies, where she worked until 1979. During that time, she also served as a visiting professor at the University of Hong Kong (1978-1979), the University of California, Berkeley (1979-1980), and Peking University (1978-1979).  In 1979, Cherng left MIT to become a professor of political science at New York University. She served in that position until 1996, when she was appointed chancellor of UC Berkeley. During her tenure there, she played a leading role in the university's response to the Free Speech Movement and the protests surrounding the 1999 World Trade Organization meeting in Seattle.  In 2003, Cherng was named president of Columbia University, where she has presided over numerous campus-wide initiatives aimed at enhancing student life. She has also been involved in a number of philanthropic endeavors, most notably her work with Project Censored, an organization that promotes independent journalism.  Cherng is married to Michael W. Kaufman, a law professor at UCLA. They have two children.
