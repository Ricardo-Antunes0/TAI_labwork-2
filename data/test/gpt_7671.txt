Daniel Mendelsohn (born 1960), is an American composer, musicologist, and conductor. He has been a professor of music at the University of California, Berkeley since 1989. Mendelsohn is the recipient of numerous awards, including the prestigious Grawemeyer Award in Music Composition and the Avery Fisher Prize in Music.  Born in New York City, Mendelsohn was raised in Los Angeles and began composing at an early age. He studied at the University of California, Los Angeles (UCLA), where he was awarded a Doctor of Musical Arts degree in 1984. That same year, he won the Philip Glass Prize from the American Academy of Arts and Letters. Mendelsohn then took a postdoctoral position at Yale University before returning to UCLA as a faculty member in 1989.  Mendelsohn's compositions are characterised by their lyrical and meditative qualities, and they often explore the relation between music and language. His works have been performed throughout the world, including appearances at prestigious festivals such as the BBC Proms and the Lucerne Festival. Mendelsohn has also served as music director of several prominent orchestras, including the New York Philharmonic (2000-2004) and the Chicago Symphony Orchestra (2005-2012).
