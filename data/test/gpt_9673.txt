Melville Weston Fuller (February 11, 1833 – July 28, 1908) was an American jurist and legal scholar who served as a justice of the Supreme Court of the United States from 1881 to his death.  Fuller was born in Morristown, New Jersey, to Nathaniel Fuller (1796–1868) and Maria Weston (née Dayton) Fuller. He was educated at Phillips Exeter Academy and Yale College, where he graduated in 1855. After graduation, he studied law with Judge Advocate General Jeremiah S. Black and was admitted to the bar in 1858.  Fuller began his legal career as a lawyer in private practice in Boston, Massachusetts. In 1861 he joined the Union Army as a lieutenant colonel of the 27th Massachusetts Infantry Regiment and served in the American Civil War. He was brevetted colonel for gallantry at the Battle of Gettysburg and brigadier general for distinguished service during the Siege of Petersburg. After the war, he resumed his legal career in Boston.  In 1871 Fuller was nominated by President Ulysses S. Grant to a seat on the United States District Court for the District of Massachusetts vacated by John Davis Longsworth. Fuller was confirmed by the United States Senate and received his commission on January 10, 1871. He served on the court until his appointment to the Supreme Court, which he received on April 30, 1881.
