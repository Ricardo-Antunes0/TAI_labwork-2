Sumner was born in Washington, D.C., and attended the University of Virginia, from which he received his bachelor's degree in 1917 and his law degree in 1919. He served in World War I as a captain in the United States Army Reserve, where he attained the rank of major. Sumner practiced law in Washington, D.C., from 1919 to 1942. He was twice elected to the American Bar Association, serving as its president in 1940 and 1941.  Sumner was nominated to the Supreme Court by President Franklin D. Roosevelt on August 5, 1939, and was confirmed by the United States Senate on August 8, 1939, becoming the youngest justice on the court at age 49. He served on the court until his retirement on October 10, 1960. Sumner is best known for his writing on constitutional law and legal interpretation, and for his dissents from majority opinions in key cases such as "Brown v. Board of Education" (1954) and " Gideon v. Wainwright" (1963).  Sumner died at age 78 of a heart attack in Bethesda, Maryland.
