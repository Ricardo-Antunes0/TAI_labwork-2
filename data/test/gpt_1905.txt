Edythe Danick London (born September 14, 1948) is an American artist, sculptor, and musician. She is known for her large-scale installations and sculptures, her collaborations with architects and engineers, and for her contributions to the fields of performance art, video art, and sound art.  London was born in 1948 in Los Angeles, California. She received her BFA from the University of California, Los Angeles in 1971. She studied at the California Institute of the Arts from 1971 to 1974, where she was awarded a scholarship to study with Joseph Beuys. In 1975 she moved to New York City, where she continued her studies at the School of Visual Arts. That same year she had a solo exhibition at the Leo Castelli Gallery.  London has had numerous solo exhibitions throughout her career, including at the Museum of Modern Art (New York City), the Whitney Museum of American Art (New York City), the Museum of Contemporary Art (Los Angeles), and the Tate Britain (London). Her works are in numerous public collections, including the Metropolitan Museum of Art (New York City), the Museum of Modern Art (Los Angeles), and the Victoria and Albert Museum (London).  London has collaborated with a range of architects and engineers throughout her career, most notably Rolf Disch with whom she created "The Cage" (1977), an iconic installation composed of a metal cage hung from the ceiling that visitors are free to walk or climb inside.
