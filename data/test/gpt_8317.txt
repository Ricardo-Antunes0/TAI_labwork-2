She was the first woman to graduate from the University of Michigan and the first woman to earn a law degree in the United States. In 1875, Sherman became the first woman to be elected to the United States Congress.  Born in Cincinnati, Ohio, Sherman attended the Cincinnati Female Seminary until she was eighteen years old. She then studied at two universities in Europe before returning to America and enrolling at the University of Michigan in 1849. There she completed her undergraduate studies in three years and earned her law degree in 1852. After graduation, she opened a law practice in Detroit, Michigan.  Sherman became involved in social reform while practicing law. In 1855 she founded the Female Reform Association of Detroit, which advocated for women's suffrage, education, and labor rights. That same year she served as president of the National Woman Suffrage Association. In 1869 she founded the Women's Trade Union League (WTUL), which became one of the largest women's organizations in the United States. Sherman also served as president of the National Education Association (NEA) from 1870 to 1871 and as president of the National Temperance Union (NTU) from 1886 to 1888.  In 1875 Sherman was elected to Congress as a Democrat from Michigan's 6th congressional district. She served until 1889, when she retired due to poor health.
