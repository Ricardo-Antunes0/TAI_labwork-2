Arthur Thomas (Spence) Powell (8 August 1903 – 24 October 1989) was an American statesman who served as the 41st United States Secretary of State (1969–1973), the first African American to hold that office.  Powell was born in Harlem, New York City, to Jamaican-born parents. Powell attended the City College of New York and later Yale Law School, where he received his J.D. in 1925. Powell then served as a law clerk for Supreme Court Justice Louis D. Brandeis. He entered politics as a Republican, and was elected to the U.S. House of Representatives from New York's 12th District in 1944. He served two terms in Congress, then became Governor of New York in 1958.  In 1960, Powell was nominated by Republican President Dwight D. Eisenhower to be Secretary of State, becoming the first African American to hold that office. As Secretary of State, Powell promoted human rights and international cooperation, and worked to resolve the Vietnam War. He played a significant role in the development of U.S.-Soviet relations during the Cold War era. After leaving government service in 1973, Powell continued to work on human rights issues and served as chairman of The World Bank from 1981 to 1985. He retired from public life in 1985, but remained active in promoting civil rights and international cooperation until his death eight years later at the age of 87.
