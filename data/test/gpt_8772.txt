Camp Wadsworth  was a World War II-era United States Army training camp located in the Catskill Mountains in the Town of Peekskill, New York. The camp was used to train soldiers for combat in Europe and the Pacific Theater of Operations.  The camp was named after Major General Leonard T. "Swede" Wadsworth, who was a senior officer during World War I and World War II. The original camp site was acquired in 1941 by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, who began construction on the camp the following year. The first troops arrived at Camp Wadsworth on October 15, 1942.  During World War II, Camp Wadsworth served as a training ground for thousands of soldiers, including members of the U.S. Army's 101st Airborne Division and the 82nd Airborne Division, as well as other Allied forces such as the British 1st Airborne Division and Canadian 2nd Infantry Division. The camp also hosted training programs for artillery, engineering, signals, medical, and other support troops.  After World War II ended, Camp Wadsworth was closed in 1946 and turned over to the New York National Guard. The camp remained inactive until 1981, when it was re-opened as a National Guard training base. In 1994, Camp Wadsworth became a joint military training facility operated by the New York National Guard and U.S. Army Reserve; it is now known as Joint Base Lewis-McChord.
