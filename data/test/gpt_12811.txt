Liebe zu Böhmen (1972) is an autobiography of the Czech author Václav Havel. Written in Czech, it tells the story of Havel's upbringing in Communist Czechoslovakia and his political development during the 1968 Prague Spring. The book has been translated into over 20 languages.  Havel was born in 1935 in Prague, Czechoslovakia, to a family of lawyers. He studied law at Charles University and joined the Communist Party of Czechoslovakia in 1956. In 1967 he was arrested for participating in the Prague Spring, an attempted reform of Communism in Czechoslovakia. He was held for seven months before being released and fled to West Germany, where he continued his political career. Havel became president of the Czech Republic in 1989 after the fall of the Communist dictatorship. He resigned in 2003 after being elected President of the European Parliament. Havel is a prominent advocate for civil rights and human rights and has been awarded numerous accolades, including the Presidential Medal of Freedom from George W. Bush in 2005 and the Nobel Peace Prize in 1994.
