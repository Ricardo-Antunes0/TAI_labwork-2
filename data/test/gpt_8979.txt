Elisabeth Freeman (September 12, 1876 – February 7, 1954) was an American psychologist and founding member of the Association for Psychological Science. She was also the first woman to receive a doctorate in psychology from Yale University.  Freeman was born on September 12, 1876 in Worcester, Massachusetts to Charles Freeman and Lavinia (née Hill) Freeman. She attended public schools in Worcester before enrolling at Vassar College in 1895. There, she majored in mathematics and psychology under the guidance of William James.  Freeman received her bachelor's degree from Vassar in 1898 and her doctorate from Yale University in 1902. At Yale, she was mentored by Edward Thorndike and graduated with a thesis on "The Nature of Aesthetic Perception".  After completing her doctorate, Freeman worked as a research associate at the National Bureau of Economic Research and as a lecturer at Bryn Mawr College. In 1911, she became an instructor at Smith College and remained there until 1920. That year, she became an associate professor at the University of California, Berkeley. From 1927 to 1935, she served as the chair of the department of psychology at Bryn Mawr College.  Freeman retired from academia in 1935 but continued to work until her death on February 7, 1954. During her career, she published more than 100 articles and books on topics including perception, mental testing, intelligence, personality development, and psychoanalysis.
