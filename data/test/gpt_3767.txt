Walter C. Kaiser Jr. (born April 11, 1927) is an American businessman, engineer, and politician. He served as the 38th Governor of California from 1967 to 1975 and as a U.S. Senator from 1975 to 1981.  Kaiser was born in Fresno, California, to Walter C. Kaiser Sr., an engineer and entrepreneur, and his wife, Flora (n√©e McDonald). His paternal grandfather was German immigrant Carl Wilhelm Kaiser, founder of the C. W. Kaiser company. After graduating from high school in 1945, he enrolled at the University of California, Berkeley, where he earned a bachelor's degree in engineering in 1949. Kaiser then served in the United States Army as a lieutenant during the Korean War.  Kaiser later became a corporate engineer for Hewlett-Packard and then a vice president at General Motors before founding his own engineering firm, Kaiser Engineers, in 1958. In 1967 he was elected governor of California on the Republican ticket, becoming the first African American to be elected to statewide office in the United States. During his tenure, he oversaw major changes in education policy and infrastructure development while combating an opioid epidemic that had begun to sweep the state. In 1981 he was elected to the U.S. Senate on the Republican ticket, serving until his retirement in 1995. He currently serves as chairman emeritus of Kaiser Permanente and is a trustee of Stanford University and USC School of Dental Medicine.
