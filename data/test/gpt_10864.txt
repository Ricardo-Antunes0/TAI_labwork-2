After graduating from high school, he attended Oberlin College, where he earned his A.B. in 1921. Alvord then spent two years at Wesleyan University, receiving his M.A. in 1923. He then attended Princeton University, where he earned his Ph.D. in 1928 under the supervision of Irving Fisher.  After completing his doctorate, Alvord worked as an instructor at Harvard University and then at the London School of Economics (LSE). In 1933 he returned to the United States and became a professor at Brown University. He also served as president of the American Economic Association (AEA) in 1951 and 1952.  Alvord was awarded the Nobel Prize in economics in 1976 "for his pioneering work on economic theory and its application to economic policy". He shared the prize with James Tobin.  Alvord is most famous for his work on general equilibrium theory and for his development of the concept of market efficiency. He is also known for his work on price determination and market structure, as well as for his contributions to monetary theory and to public finance.
