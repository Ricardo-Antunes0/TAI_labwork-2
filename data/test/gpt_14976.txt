Gerhard Rüdiger Gundermann, who generally performed as Gerhard Gundermann, (born February 8, 1933, in Berlin, Ger.—died March 2, 2009, in Berlin) German composer and conductor of classical and popular music.  Gundermann’s early career as a composer was spent in the Federal Republic of Germany (West Germany), where he was a protégé of Hans Werner Henze. He began his orchestral career as the first conductor of the Staatskapelle Dresden (1968–72). He also served as director of the Staatstheater Nürnberg (1972–86) and the Konzerthaus Wien (1986–92). From 1992 until his death, he was a professor at the Hochschule für Musik Karlsruhe.  Gundermann’s compositions are marked by a lyrical yet rigorous approach to classical form. His best-known works include the opera Der fliegende Holländer (1986), the symphonic poem Die Erde (1984), and the oratorio Die letzten Tage der Menschheit (1992). In addition to his own works, he has championed new music by composers such as Karlheinz Stockhausen, Pierre Boulez, and Jordi Savall.
