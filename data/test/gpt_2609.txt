Georg Muschner (12 June 1885 – 17 December 1937) was an Austrian composer and conductor.  Muschner was born in Vienna, Austria, to a musical family. His father, Christian Muschner, was a violinist and conductor, and his mother, Maria Theresia (née Horn), was a pianist. He began his musical education at the age of six at the music academy of the University of Vienna, where he studied with Ignaz Moscheles. From 1907 to 1909 he studied composition with Arnold Schoenberg at the Vienna Academy of Music.  In 1911 Muschner became assistant conductor of the Zürich Opera House. In 1914 he moved to Berlin to become conductor of the Berlin State Opera. From 1918 to 1921 he served as director of the Staatliche Hochschule für Musik in Berlin. In 1922 he became conductor of the Vienna State Opera.  In 1933 Muschner fled Nazi Germany and settled in England, where he worked as a conductor for several music festivals. He died in London in 1937. He composed mainly classical music, including operas, symphonies, and chamber works. His best-known works are the opera "Lulu" (1921), the Symphony No. 1 (1925), and the String Quartet No. 2 (1930).
