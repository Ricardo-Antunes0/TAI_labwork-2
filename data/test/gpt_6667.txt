Jan Tomáš "Miloš" Forman (; ; 18 May 1928 – 18 February 2002) was a Czechoslovakian-born American filmmaker, screenwriter, and actor. He was a major figure in the New Wave film movement of the late 1960s and early 1970s, and is widely regarded as one of the greatest filmmakers of the 20th century.  Born in Zlín, Czechoslovakia (now Slovakia), Forman spent his early childhood moving around the countryside before ending up in Nazi-occupied Warsaw. After the war, he studied at the University of Warsaw before moving to Paris to study art. He made his feature film debut with "The Fireman's Ball" (1958), which won him international acclaim. In 1969, he moved to the United States, and subsequently became one of the most successful filmmakers in Hollywood, with films such as "The Graduate", "One Flew Over The Cuckoo's Nest", "The Deer Hunter", and "The Unbearable Lightness of Being" achieving worldwide critical and commercial success.  Forman was nominated for Academy Awards for Best Director for "The Elephant Man" (1980), "Amadeus" (1984), and "The People vs. Larry Flynt" (1996).
