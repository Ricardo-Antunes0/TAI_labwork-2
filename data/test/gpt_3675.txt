Frederick Ronald Williams OBE (23 January 192722 March 2004) was an English artist and sculptor. He is best known for his large-scale abstract sculptures, which often incorporated broken or discarded objects.  Williams was born in London, the son of a railway clerk. He grew up in North London and attended Hornsey School of Art from 1944 to 1948, after which he served in the Royal Air Force for two years. After his discharge, he taught at various art schools in Britain and abroad, and became a full professor at the Royal College of Art in 1977.  Williams exhibited throughout the world, including at the Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum, the Museum of Modern Art in New York City, and the Centre Pompidou in Paris. He received numerous awards, including a prestigious commission from the British government to create a large sculpture for the entrance to their new Wembley Stadium. In 2002, Williams was appointed an honorary Fellow of the Royal Academy of Arts. He died in 2004 at the age of 76.
