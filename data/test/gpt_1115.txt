Joseph Torgesen is an Emeritus Professor of Psychology at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, who has made significant contributions to the field of mental disorder research. He is best known for his work on the diagnostic criteria for schizophrenia, and for his development of the symptom-level approach to diagnosing mental disorders.  Born in 1928 in Chicago, Illinois, Torgesen received his undergraduate degree from Northwestern University in 1950 and his Ph.D. from the University of California, Berkeley, in 1955. He began his career as a research psychologist at the National Institutes of Mental Health (NIMH) in Bethesda, Maryland, where he served as director of the schizophrenia research program from 1970 to 1975. He then served as professor of psychology at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill until his retirement in 2001.  Torgesen's research has focused on the development and assessment of diagnostic criteria for schizophrenia and other mental disorders, as well as on developing and testing psychosocial treatments for those disorders. He has authored or coauthored more than 100 journal articles and book chapters, and has been awarded numerous honors including the Distinguished Scientific Contributions Award from NIMH in 1992, the American Psychological Association's Division 51 Award for outstanding contributions to forensic psychology in 1993, and the prestigious Karl Jaspers Prize from the European Psychiatric Association in 2004.
