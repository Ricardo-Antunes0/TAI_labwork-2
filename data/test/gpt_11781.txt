Edith Hope Ogden Heidel (February 8, 1870 – February 25, 1945) was an American author, philosopher, and feminist.  Heidel was born in Worcester, Massachusetts, to Hope (née Winchell) and Lewis Heidel. Her father was a Congregational minister. She attended Radcliffe College, where she studied philosophy and theology. In 1895 she married philosopher Alfred North Whitehead, with whom she had two children. The marriage ended in divorce in 1912.  Heidel then moved to Europe, where she lived for the rest of her life. She was a professor at the University of Freiburg in Germany from 1913 to 1914 and at the University of Munich from 1914 to 1918. In 1918 she returned to the United States and began teaching at Bryn Mawr College. She retired in 1944.  Heidel wrote prolifically on a range of philosophical and religious topics. Her best-known works are The Metaphysical Foundations of Logic (1909), The Philosophy of Religion (1912), and Creative Intelligence (1937). She also wrote extensively on feminist issues, including essays entitled "The Problem of Woman" (1908) and "The Psychology of Women" (1928).  Heidel died in 1945 in Bryn Mawr after a long illness.
