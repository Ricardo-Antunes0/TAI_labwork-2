Barack Hussein Obama Sr. (; 18 June 1961 â€“ 18 October 2008), also known as Barry Soetoro, was an American politician who served as the 44th President of the United States from 2009 to 2017. A member of the Democratic Party, he was the first African American to be elected to the presidency.  Obama was born in Honolulu, Hawaii, two years after his parents had emigrated from his father's native Kenya to the United States. He was raised in Honolulu and Washington, D.C., and attended Columbia University and Harvard Law School, where he was a member of the Law Review. Obama worked as a civil rights attorney and political organizer in Chicago before being elected to the U.S. Senate in 2004. He became president in 2009 when he won the general election against Republican John McCain. Eight months into his first term, Obama was attacked by a self-identified terrorist named Osama bin Laden in retaliation for United States military actions in Afghanistan, leading to a prolonged military campaign dubbed Operation Neptune's Spear. In May 2010, Obama announced that he would seek re-election to a second term in office; he was re-elected by a wide margin in November 2012. During his second term, Obama signed into law major economic reforms including the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act (ACA), which became known as Obamacare after passage in March 2010.
