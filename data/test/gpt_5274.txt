Kerry Harrison Cook is an American climate scientist and the Director of the Program on Climate Change at Columbia University's Earth Institute. He has also been a professor at Columbia University, where he served as the director of the earth system science program from 2002 to 2006.  Cook is known for his work on climate change, its causes and effects, and its potential consequences for human societies. He has written or edited more than 150 scientific papers, articles, and books on climate change. He is a member of the National Academy of Sciences, the American Academy of Arts and Sciences, and the Royal Society of London.  Born in Boston, Massachusetts in 1963, Cook grew up in suburban Newton. After graduating from Newton South High School in 1981, he attended Tufts University, where he obtained a B.A. in mathematics in 1985. He then attended the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), where he obtained a M.S. in atmospheric science in 1986 and a Ph.D. in climatology in 1990.  Cook began his career as a research scientist at MIT's Center for Atmosphere Research (CER). He was one of the first scientists to develop sophisticated models to study global climate change. In 1992 he moved to Columbia University's Earth Institute as an assistant professor of earth system science. He was promoted to associate professor in 1995 and professor in 2002, becoming the director of the earth system science program at Columbia University.
