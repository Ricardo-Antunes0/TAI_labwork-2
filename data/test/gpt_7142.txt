Sir Arthur Ernest Hawkins   (10 October 1853 – 24 March 1922) was a British diplomat and colonial administrator. He was Governor of the Gold Coast from 1899 to 1903 and Governor General of the Union of South Africa from 1907 to 1910.  Hawkins was born in 1853, the son of Arthur Hawkins, a surgeon, and his wife Emma. He was educated at Eton and Trinity College, Cambridge, where he graduated with a Bachelor of Arts in 1877.  After university, Hawkins entered the Colonial Service as a clerk in the Foreign Office. He served in various posts in Africa, including manager of the Upper Nile Expedition (1884–85), Secretary to the Bechuanaland Protectorate (1885–87), and fourth secretary at the British Legation, Cairo (1887–89). In 1889 he was appointed consul-general at Cape Town, South Africa.  In 1899 Hawkins was appointed governor of the Gold Coast, a post he held until 1903. During his time in office, he oversaw extensive development work on the colony, including construction of roads, bridges and hospitals. He also promulgated various reforms that aimed to improve the lives of Africans living on the Gold Coast.  In 1907 Hawkins was appointed governor general of the Union of South Africa, a post he held until 1910.
