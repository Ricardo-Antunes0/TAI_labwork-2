Charles Henry Tweed (June 24, 1895 – August 17, 1934) was an American politician and businessman who served as the 34th Governor of New York (1928–1934). He was also the leader of Tammany Hall, one of the most powerful political machines in the country. Tweed was convicted of corruption in 1931 and was sent to prison. He died in prison.  Tweed was born in West Point, New York, son of a poor Irish immigrant father and a mother who was of Dutch descent. After graduating from high school, he worked as a bootblack and then a janitor. In 1917, he married Cora Kelly and they had two children.  Tweed began his political career as a reformer on the city council of his hometown. In 1923, he was elected to the New York State Assembly, where he became known for his aggressive attacks on corrupt politicians. In 1928, Tweed was elected governor on a platform that advocated reform of the state's corrupt election system and aggressive prosecution of crime.  As governor, Tweed became known for his lavish lifestyle and his close relationships with powerful business leaders. His administration was rocked by allegations of corruption, which culminated in his conviction for bribery and extortion in 1931. After serving 18 months in prison, Tweed died from a heart attack while on parole in 1934.
