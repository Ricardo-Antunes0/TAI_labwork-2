Lawrence George Bernard (February 9, 1914 – February 10, 1991) was an American philosopher and writer who specialized in the philosophy of science.  Bernard was born in New York City, the son of Clara (née Bicknell) and Lawrence Bernard, a lawyer. He received his B.A. from Harvard University in 1935 and his M.A. from the University of Chicago in 1936. He then went to Oxford University, where he received his D.Phil. in 1938. Returning to the United States, he became an instructor at the University of Chicago and then an assistant professor at Princeton University. He became a full professor at the University of California, Berkeley, in 1965 and remained at Berkeley until his death in 1991.  Bernard was a major figure in the philosophy of science for his work on the philosophy of mathematics, especially for his book The Philosophy of Mathematics (1953). Other significant works include Scientific Explanation (1951), The Nature of Mathematical Truth (1956), and The Philosophy of Physics (1969). He also wrote articles on various other topics in philosophy and science, including epistemology, semantics, metaphysics, and aesthetics.  Bernard was elected a Fellow of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences in 1975. He was awarded the National Medal of Science by President Jimmy Carter in 1980.
