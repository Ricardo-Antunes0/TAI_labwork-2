Samuel Loudon (1727–1813)  was a colonial American jurist and politician who served as the eighth Governor of New Hampshire from 1790 to 1792.  Born in Londonderry, New Hampshire, Loudon was the son of Judge Samuel Loudon and his wife Sarah Burr. He was educated at Dartmouth College and then read law with his father. He was admitted to the New Hampshire Bar in 1749 and commenced practice in Londonderry.  Loudon was a member of the New Hampshire House of Representatives from 1766 to 1768, and was a delegate to the Continental Congress from New Hampshire in 1774. He was a judge on the Court of Common Pleas from 1777 to 1792, and was also a member of the New Hampshire Senate from 1778 to 1792.  In 1790, Loudon was elected Governor of New Hampshire, and served until his resignation in 1792. During his tenure, Loudon signed into law legislation creating a state constitutional government, established the University of New Hampshire, and enacted measures to improve the economy. He also presided over the trial of John Peter Zenger.  Loudon died in Londonderry in 1813. He was buried in the Loudon family plot at Northwood Cemetery in Londonderry.
