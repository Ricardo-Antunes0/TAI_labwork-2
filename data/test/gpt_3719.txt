Andrew Adgate Lipscomb (September 5, 1816 â€“ October 9, 1892) was an American lawyer and jurist who served as the sixth Chief Justice of the United States from 1881 to 1887.  Born in Nashville, Tennessee, Lipscomb was the son of Andrew A. Lipscomb and Mary Adgate. He received an A.B. from the University of Virginia in 1837 and an LL.B. from the University of Nashville in 1840. He commenced practice in Nashville, Tennessee, and was a member of the Tennessee House of Representatives from 1851 to 1853. He was a delegate to the Democratic National Convention in 1856 and a member of the United States Senate from 1860 to 1868.  Lipscomb was nominated by President James Buchanan on December 5, 1868, to a seat on the United States Supreme Court vacated by John Marshall Harlan II. He was confirmed by the United States Senate on December 8, 1868, and received his commission on December 9, 1968. He served as Chief Justice from January 3, 1881, to January 3, 1887. In 1886 he delivered a famous speech in which he argued that corporations are people under the law. He retired from the Supreme Court on January 3, 1888.  Lipscomb died in Nashville, Tennessee, aged 83 years, 174 days. He is interred at Forest Hill Cemetery there.
