Carl Eugene Moses (July 16, 1929 – March 25, 2006) was an American civil rights activist, author, and lecturer. He was a prominent figure in the American Civil Rights Movement and one of the earliest and most effective spokespersons for black America. He served as the first African American to be elected as chairman of the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP).  Moses was born in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, to Quaker parents. He attended Philadelphia’s Germantown Friends School and then went on to study at Howard University, where he earned a B.A. in political science in 1951. After completing his undergraduate studies, Moses served in the U.S. Air Force from 1951 to 1953.  Following his military service, Moses worked as a field representative for the NAACP in Cleveland, Ohio. In 1962, he became the organization’s regional director for the Midwest region. In this position, Moses helped lead the fight against segregation in schools and public facilities throughout Ohio and Indiana.  In 1965, Moses was appointed director of the NAACP’s Southern division. Under his leadership, the Southern division increased its membership from 1,000 to 10,000 and launched a number of successful campaigns against racial discrimination. In 1968, Moses was elected chairman of the NAACP. During his tenure, he worked to improve relations between the NAACP and other civil rights organizations and led efforts to increase membership among black Americans nationwide.
