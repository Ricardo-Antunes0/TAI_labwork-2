Gabrielle Bonheur "Coco" Chanel ( ; 19 August 1892 – 8 January 1971) was a French fashion designer and businesswoman who is credited with helping to create the Chanel brand.  Chanel was a leading figure in the fashion world during the 1920s and 1930s. She was a pioneering fashion designer who created clothing for women that was functional, stylish, and eye-catching. Her Chanel No. 5 perfume is one of the most popular fragrances in the world.  Chanel was born in Paris to a wealthy family of wine merchants. As a child, she showed an interest in fashion and design, and she began making her own clothes at the age of twelve. In 1909, she opened her own fashion salon in Paris, which became a popular attraction due to Chanel's innovative designs and her insistence on high quality standards.  During World War I (1914–1918), Chanel worked as a nurse in a military hospital in northern France. After the war, she resumed her career as a fashion designer and began to achieve international recognition. In 1923, she founded her own company, Chanel couture, which became one of the leading fashion brands in the world. Chanel played an important role in popularizing French style throughout the world and she was instrumental in bringing modernity and elegance to women's wardrobes.  Chanel died in 1971 at the age of 84 years old. She remains one of the most iconic and influential figures in fashion history.
