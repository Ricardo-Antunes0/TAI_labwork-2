Frank Rattray Lillie (June 27, 1870November 5, 1943) was an American composer, conductor, and music educator.  Lillie was born in Knoxville, Tennessee, to a musical family. His father, James Rattray Lillie, was a composer and pianist. The younger Lillie began his musical studies at the age of six, studying piano with his father and later composition with Daniel Gregory Mason. He graduated from the University of Michigan in 1892 with a degree in music.  Lillie began his career as a composer and conductor. He served as the director of music at several institutions, including Wells College (Worcester, Massachusetts) and the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill. In 1912 he became the first professor of music at the newly founded Smith College. In 1923 he moved to the University of California, Berkeley, where he served as professor of music until his retirement in 1939.  Lillie's compositions include orchestral works, chamber music, songs, and pieces for solo instruments. His notable works include the opera "The Bohemian Girl" (1916), the symphonic poem "The Planets" (1918), and the orchestral suite "East Coker" (1920). He also wrote several pedagogical texts on music theory and composition.
