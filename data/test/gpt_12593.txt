Joia Stapleton Mukherjee (born 1964) is an Indian-American physician and scientist who is the first woman to become a tenured professor at Harvard Medical School. She is also the first woman to be appointed as director of the National Institutes of Health.  Mukherjee was born in Calcutta, India, in 1964. Her father, Stapleton Mukherjee, was an obstetrician-gynecologist, and her mother, Sarojini Naidu, was a writer and education activist. She has two sisters and one brother. Mukherjee attended GEMS School in Dubai from 1971 to 1978 and then studied medicine at the University of Cambridge, where she graduated with a degree in medicine in 1983. She then completed her residency at Brigham and Women's Hospital in Boston, Massachusetts.  Mukherjee began her career as a research associate at the Harvard Medical School in 1985. In 1994, she became an assistant professor at the school and was promoted to associate professor in 1998 and full professor in 2003. She was the first woman to become a tenured professor at Harvard Medical School. In 2009, she became the first woman to be appointed as director of the National Institutes of Health (NIH). During her tenure as NIH director, Mukherjee oversaw major medical research initiatives such as the BRAIN Initiative and the President's Cancer Panel Report.  Mukherjee has received numerous awards throughout her career, including the Presidential Early Career Award for Scientists and Engineers in 2001 and the Albert Einstein World Award
