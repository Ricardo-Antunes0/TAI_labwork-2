Vincent Malo or Vincent Malo I (Cambrai, 10 January 1898 – Paris, 16 January 1976) was a French painter and sculptor. Malo was a leading member of the Fauves movement in painting in the 1920s.  Born into a bourgeois family, Malo spent much of his early childhood in Belgium before returning to France with his family in 1905. He initially studied engineering at the École Polytechnique, but abandoned this to study art at the Académie Julian in Paris, where he was taught by Paul Cézanne and Henri Matisse. During World War I, Malo served in the French Army as a captain. After the war, he resumed his studies at the Académie Julian, this time under Raoul Dufy.  Malo's early paintings were abstract and expressionistic, but he soon began to experiment with different techniques, including Pointillism and Fauvism. He joined the Club des Indépendants in 1921 and exhibited his work there from 1922 to 1924. In 1926, he exhibited with the group known as Les Nabis at the Galerie de l'Effort Moderne, which helped to promote his work.  In the early 1930s, Malo began working on a series of paintings known as the Rouault Series, after the French artist Georges Rouault.
