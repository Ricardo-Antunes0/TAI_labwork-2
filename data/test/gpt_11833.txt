He is known for his work on the theory of the electron, nuclear reactions, and the Long-wave length.  Oren E. Long was born in 1889 in New York City. He received his bachelor's degree from UC Berkeley in 1911 and his doctorate from Columbia University in 1915. He worked at Princeton University from 1915 to 1916 and at the University of Chicago from 1916 to 1917. In 1917 he moved to the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton, where he remained until his retirement in 1957.  Long was a fellow of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences (1935), the American Physical Society (1945), and the Royal Society of London (1950). He was also a president of the American Physical Society (1948), the National Academy of Sciences (1957), and the International Union of Pure and Applied Physics (1959).  Long was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1954 for his "discoveries concerning nuclear reactions and the properties of subatomic particles, including the electron".
