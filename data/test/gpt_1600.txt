Warner Bros. Entertainment Inc. (commonly known as Warner Bros.) is an American entertainment conglomerate founded in 1919 by Harry Warner and Albert Warner. The company has since been the leading producer of motion pictures and television programs, as well as music recordings. Warner Bros. is a member of the Motion Picture Association of America (MPAA), and its flagship division is Warner Bros. Pictures, Inc., which oversees all of its film and television studios.  Warner Bros. originated in the late 1920s, when two brothers, Harry and Albert Warner, started a small film projection business in Boston. In the 1930s, they moved to New York City and joined the growing industry of Hollywood. Albert's younger brother Jack joined them in 1939, and the three began what would become one of the most influential Hollywood studios.  The company produced its first feature-length movie, "The Jazz Singer", in 1927. In the 1940s, it produced the critically acclaimed "A Streetcar Named Desire" and "The Maltese Falcon". In 1953, Harry Warner retired and sold his interest in the company to his two sons, Ted and Bob. The latter became president of the studio, while the former became vice-president. In 1966, following a legal dispute with their sister company 20th Century Fox over film credits for "The Gold Rush", Warner Bros. merged with Seven Arts Productions, creating Seven Arts Motion Picture Corporation.
