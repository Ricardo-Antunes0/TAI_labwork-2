Bernard Mannes Baruch (August 19, 1870 – January 30, 1965) was an American businessman and politician who served as the eighteenth United States Secretary of Commerce (1917–1921), and as a Senator from New York (1921–1933). He also served as the Republican National Committee chairman from 1924 to 1928.  Born in New York City, Baruch attended the City College of New York, where he was a member of the Delta Kappa Epsilon fraternity. In 1894, he founded the brokerage firm of Bernard Baruch and Company. He later served as its president and chairman. He also served as a director of several major companies, including the General Electric Company and International Business Machines Corporation.  Baruch was a delegate to the 1932 Republican National Convention, where he was nominated for Vice President on the ticket with Herbert Hoover. They lost to Franklin D. Roosevelt and John F. Kennedy in the election. After leaving office, Baruch became a professor at Columbia University and a spokesman for UNICEF. He died in 1965 in New York City at the age of 100 years old.
