Humphrey Forman (26 April 1888 – 21 February 1976) was an English stage and film director, producer, screenwriter and actor. He is considered one of the most influential and successful directors in British cinema.  Born in London, Humphrey Forman began his career as an actor and stage manager before making his directorial debut with the short film "The Horse's Mouth" (1932). He gained wider recognition for his features films such as "A Passage to India" (1951), "Cambridge Circus" (1953), "The Jazz Singer" (1927) and "Cabaret" (1972), which were all nominated for the Academy Award for Best Picture.  He also directed several successful television series, including "The Prisoner" (1967–68), "Doctor Who" (1963–69), "The Waltons" (1968–73) and "Minder" (1979–82). He retired from filmmaking in 1982, but continued to work as a producer until his death in 1976.
