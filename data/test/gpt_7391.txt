Alfred Trzebinski (29 August 1902 – 8 January 1966) was a Polish film director and screenwriter. He is considered one of the most influential filmmakers of the Polish New Wave.  Born in 1902 in Warsaw, Trzebinski grew up in a middle-class family. In 1925 he graduated from the Warsaw School of Painting and Sculpture, where he met Krzysztof Kieślowski and Andrzej Wajda. He started his career as an illustrator and later worked as an art director for films. He made his directorial debut with the war film "The Captive" (1939), which was followed by "The Ascent" (1941) and "The Battle of Piaśnica" (1943).  In the late 1940s he directed a number of acclaimed films, including "The Devil's Playground" (1948), "The Return" (1948), "No Way Out" (1950), "A Short Film About Killing" (1958) and "Black Friday" (1960). His later films include "The Wedding March" (1963), "Once Upon a Time in Poland" (1970), and "The Night Porter" (1974). Trzebinski died in 1966 in Warsaw.
