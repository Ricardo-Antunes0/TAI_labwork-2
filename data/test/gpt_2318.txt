He is best known for his work in the theory of financial markets, in particular for developing the efficient market hypothesis.  Tett was born on October 16, 1916, in Baltimore, Maryland. He received his B.A. from Brown University in 1937 and his Ph.D. from Harvard University in 1942. He began his academic career at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, where he served as a professor from 1949 until his retirement in 1984. He also held a visiting professorship at the University of California, Berkeley, from 1967 to 1968.  Tett's work in financial markets has had a profound impact on economic theory and on the practice of investment and finance. His seminal work on the efficient market hypothesis has become one of the most well-known and widely accepted theories in economics.  Tett was also a highly esteemed teacher. He was a popular lecturer at both MIT and Princeton, where he served as chairman of the department of economics at Princeton University from 1972 to 1978. In 1978 he was awarded the Nobel Prize in Economics "for his fundamental work in the theory of financial markets."  Tett died on October 7, 1984, in Cambridge, Massachusetts.
