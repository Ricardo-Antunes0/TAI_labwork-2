Boddington was born in London, the son of a surgeon, and educated at Westminster School and Christ Church, Oxford. He became a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1763. In 1775 he was appointed physician to the Prince of Wales. He served as President of the Royal Society from 1795 to 1802.   Boddington is most notable for his work in chemistry, particularly his investigations into the nature of acids and bases. He is also known for his work on distillation, in particular his development of the Boddington column still. Boddington was also a noted botanist, and made contributions to the understanding of plant physiology.   Boddington died in London at the age of 78.
