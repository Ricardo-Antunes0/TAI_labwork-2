Haiti (; French:  ;  ), officially the Republic of Haiti (; French:  ), is a country located in the Greater Antilles in the Caribbean region. It occupies the western two-thirds of the island of Hispaniola, which it shares with the Dominican Republic.  The country was originally inhabited by the Taíno people, who migrated from South America. The first European to set foot on present-day Haiti was Christopher Columbus in 1492. After several hundred years of Spanish and French rule, Haiti became an independent nation under the leadership of Jean-Jacques Dessalines in 1804. The first president, Faustin Soulouque, was overthrown in a military coup in 1849 and replaced by Jules Dupuy.  In 1915, during World War I, Haiti became a colony of France as a result of a treaty between France and the United States. Following World War II, Haiti became a republic and elected its first president, José Hipólito Mejía Villegas. In 2006, the military junta led by Lieutenant General Raoul Cédras overthrew President Mejía Villegas and held elections in which Jean-Bertrand Aristide was elected president. In 2010, after a year of protests and political instability, President Aristide was removed from office by the military in a coup d'état.
