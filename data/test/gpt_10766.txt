Sir Davidge Gould GCB (1758 – 23 December 1827) was an English jurist and politician who sat in the House of Commons from 1796 to 1802 and from 1807 to 1827.  Gould was born at Croydon, the son of the Rev. Davidge Gould, rector of Fingringhoe, Essex. He was educated at Eton and at Christ Church, Oxford, where he graduated BA in 1783 and MA in 1787. He became a barrister at Lincoln's Inn in 1786.   Gould entered parliament as member for Croydon at a by-election on 7 January 1796. He served until his defeat at the general election in 1802. At the general election in 1807 he was returned for Maidstone, and served until his death aged 73 years.   Gould is best known for writing "The History of the Criminal Law of England from its Origins to the Year 1800" (1803–1804), which is regarded as one of the most important works on English criminal law. He also wrote "A Treatise on the Law of Probate" (1809), "Political Observations" (1820), "A Critical View of the Roman Law" (2 vols., 1821), and "An Essay on the Nature and Effects of Property" (1822).
