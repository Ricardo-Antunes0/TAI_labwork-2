Thousands of years before Europeans arrived, a tribe of Native Americans inhabited what is now Miami. In 1565, a Spanish explorer named Pánfilo de Narváez sailed up the Miami River and claimed the area for Spain.   During the early years of Spanish rule in Florida, the Miami area was sparsely populated, and the Spanish built few settlements in the area. In 1763, a group of British settlers led by James Grant arrived in what is now Miami. The British quickly established a settlement in the area and began to trade with the Native Americans. In 1783, Spain ceded Florida to the United States as part of the Treaty of Paris following the American Revolutionary War. The US government divided Florida into East and West Florida, and Miami became part of West Florida.   The US government encouraged settlement in Miami by offering land grants to anyone who would move to the area. By 1800, Miami had become a prosperous town with a population of about 1,000 people. In 1825, Miami became a city under US rule. Throughout the remainder of the 19th century, Miami grew rapidly as an industrial center. In 1913, Miami hosted the first International Exposition (now known as World's Fair) and it became an important tourist destination.   In 1932, Miami experienced a major hurricane that destroyed much of the city's infrastructure. The city rebuilt and has since experienced significant growth both economically and culturally.
