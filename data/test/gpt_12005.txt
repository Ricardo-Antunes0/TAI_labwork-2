Constance Dorothy Evelyn Peel  (née Bayliff; 12 October 1892 – 3 December 1978) was an English novelist, short story writer, poet and playwright.  Peel was born in Islington, London, the daughter of the physician and writer William Bayliff and his wife, Florence née de Rothschild. She grew up in a large family with five brothers and sisters. After attending Girton College, Cambridge, she married the novelist John Lehmann in 1919. They divorced in 1925. In 1926 she married Leonard Woolf, with whom she had two sons and a daughter. The marriage ended in 1946.  Peel was a prolific writer of novels, short stories, poetry and plays. She is best known for her wartime novel "The Years", which was published in 1948 and won the Booker Prize. Other well-known works include "The Voyage Out" (1938), "Night and Day" (1941), "To the Lighthouse" (1943), "The Years" (1948), "A Room of One's Own" (1929), "The Death of Mr. Betjeman" (1970) and "Orlando" (1964).
