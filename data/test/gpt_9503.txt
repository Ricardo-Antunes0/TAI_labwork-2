 August Wilhelm Ernst von Stockhausen (19 February 1875 – 4 July 1957) was a German composer, music theorist, and conductor. He is considered one of the most significant and influential composers of the 20th century.  Stockhausen was born in Wiesbaden, then part of the Grand Duchy of Hesse, now in Rhineland-Palatinate, Germany. His father was the lieutenant-general August von Stockhausen, and his mother was Charlotte (née Müller). In 1890, when he was nine years old, his family moved to Munich, where he began his musical studies with his father. In 1897 he began studying at the Munich Conservatory where he was a pupil of Joachim Riepel. In 1901 he won first prize in the piano competition of the Royal College of Music in London.  In 1902 Stockhausen took up an appointment as professor at the Cologne Conservatory, where he remained until 1919. During this time he composed his first major work, Klavierstück X (1906–07), which utilized extended tonality and non-traditional rhythms. In 1913 he founded the Akademie für Musik in Berlin, which became one of Europe's leading music academies.  During World War I (1914–18) Stockhausen served in the military as an artillery officer.
