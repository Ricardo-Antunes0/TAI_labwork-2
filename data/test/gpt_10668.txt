William Edward Quine (February 9, 1847 – October 26, 1950) was an American philosopher who is widely considered one of the most influential thinkers of the twentieth century. He is known for his work in logic, philosophy of language, and philosophy of science.  Quine was born in Ithaca, New York, the son of Samuel Peters Quine and Eliza Ann (née Ellis) Quine. His father was a professor of mathematics at Cornell University and his mother was a teacher. Quine graduated from Cornell in 1869, and then studied at the University of Berlin where he became interested in philosophy. He returned to the United States and earned his doctorate from Harvard in 1874.  After completing his doctorate, Quine taught at Harvard for two years before becoming a professor at Johns Hopkins University in 1879. He remained at Johns Hopkins for the rest of his career, retiring in 1939. While at Johns Hopkins, Quine also served as president of the American Philosophical Association (1909–10).  Quine's most famous philosophical work is his book "Two Dogmas of Empiricism" (1951), which challenges the philosophical assumptions that underlie classical empiricism. In it, he rejects epistemological realism—the view that knowledge is based on observation—and metaphysical realism—the view that there are fundamental truths about reality that can be known through experience.
