John Augustine Chilton Hearst (born October 24, 1863) was an American businessman and newspaper publisher. He founded the Hearst Corporation, one of the largest media companies in the world.  Hearst served as the chairman of the Republican National Committee from 1928 to 1944. He was also a supporter of Franklin D. Roosevelt, who appointed him to the United States Civil Service Commission in 1938. Hearst died in 1951.  Hearst was born in San Francisco, California, to George Hearst and Phoebe Apperson Hearst. His paternal grandfather was John D. Hearst, who had founded the San Francisco Examiner in 1849. His father was a mining engineer who also worked as a newspaper publisher and ran several mines in Arizona, New Mexico and Utah. When he was two years old, his family moved to New York City, where his father started the New York Journal. Hearst attended public schools in New York City and graduated from Harvard College in 1885.  Hearst began his career as an editor for his father's paper, the Journal. In 1888 he left the Journal to found the New York Morning Journal with his younger brother Randolph Hearst. The Morning Journal became one of the most successful newspapers in America and helped make Randolph Hearst one of America's earliest multimillionaires.   In 1897 Hearst purchased a controlling interest in the Los Angeles Examiner and merged it with his Morning Journal to create the Los Angeles Examiner-Journal. He also established a number of other newspapers across America
