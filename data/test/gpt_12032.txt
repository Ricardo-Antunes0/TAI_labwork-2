Toddington is a large village and civil parish in Bedfordshire, England. It lies about 9 miles (14 km) north-west of Bedford and 10 miles (16 km) east of Luton. The population of the parish at the 2011 census was 7,604.  Toddington is first mentioned in the Domesday Book of 1086 as "Tocedintune". The name means "the farmstead or village of the tokes or slaves".  The village has a long history as a settlement, with evidence of early human activity dating back to the Mesolithic period. There is a Bronze Age round barrow located near Toddington and a Roman villa was discovered in the village in 1886.  During the Middle Ages Toddington was an important agricultural centre and, in 1295, it was granted a Royal charter by King Edward I. In 1324, Toddington joined with neighbouring villages to form the manor of Toddington, which extended over an area of some 10,000 acres (40 kmÂ²).  The village's prosperity was based on its position as a junction on important trade routes. In 1535, Toddington became part of the holdings of Sir John Luttrell and his wife, who were among England's richest landowners. The Luttrells built a number of imposing country houses in and around Toddington and their influence can still be seen in the architecture of the village today.
