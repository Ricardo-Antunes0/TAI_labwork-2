Bernard Leon Schwartz (born December 13, 1925) is an American economist and business magnate who has been the chairman and chief executive officer (CEO) of IBM since 1981.  Schwartz was born in the Bronx, New York City, the son of Jewish immigrants from Russia. He earned his bachelor's degree from the State University of New York at Buffalo in 1949, and his master's degree from Princeton University in 1950.  After serving in the United States Army during the Korean War, Schwartz began his career at IBM in 1951 as a research scientist. He rose through the ranks to become vice president and general manager of IBM's System/360 line of mainframe computers, and then president of IBM's Systems Division. In 1981, he became CEO of IBM, and under his leadership the company became one of the world's largest technology companies. Schwartz retired from IBM in 2001, but continues to serve as chairman emeritus.  Schwartz is a member of the National Academy of Engineering and a fellow of both the American Academy of Arts and Sciences and the Institute for Electrical and Electronics Engineers. He has received numerous awards, including the Presidential Medal of Freedom (1996), the Gold Medal for Industrial Achievement from the World Economic Forum (1998), and the IEEE Computer Society's prestigious Charles Babbage Award (2006).
