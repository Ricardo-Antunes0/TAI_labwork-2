Released in 1994, "Forrest Gump" is an American comedy-drama film helmed by Robert Zemeckis and penned by Eric Roth. Adapted from Winston Groom's 1986 novel of the same title, the movie stars Tom Hanks, Robin Wright, Gary Sinise, Mykelti Williamson, and Sally Field. Set in the 20th-century United States, the story revolves around Forrest Gump (portrayed by Hanks), a man from Alabama, and his life journey. Shot primarily in Georgia, North Carolina, and South Carolina from August to December 1993, the film employed extensive visual effects to integrate Hanks into historical footage and to craft various scenes. Its soundtrack echoes the different eras depicted in the narrative. Upon its release on July 6, 1994, in the U.S., "Forrest Gump" garnered widespread critical acclaim, lauding Zemeckis's direction, performances (especially those of Hanks and Sinise), visual effects, music, and screenplay. Boasting remarkable commercial success, it became the highest-grossing film of 1994 in the United States, amassing over US$678.2 million globally during its theatrical run, trailing only "The Lion King." The accompanying soundtrack sold more than 12 million copies. At the Academy Awards, "Forrest Gump" clinched six Oscars, including Best Picture, Best Director, Best Actor for Hanks, Best Adapted Screenplay, Best Visual Effects, and Best Film Editing. It garnered numerous other award nominations, spanning Golden Globes, British Academy Film Awards, and Screen Actors Guild Awards. The film has sparked diverse interpretations regarding its protagonist and political undertones. In recognition of its cultural and historical significance, the Library of Congress inducted "Forrest Gump" into the United States National Film Registry in 2011.
